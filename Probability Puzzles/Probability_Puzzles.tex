\section*{Symmetry \& Total Probability}
\begin{question}
Two gamblers A and B have $n+1$ and $n$ fair coins respectively. If both toss
all their coins, find the probability that the number of heads of A is greater than the
number of heads of B.
\end{question}
\begin{solution}
Define events: \\
$E$: $A$ has more heads in the first $n$ tosses \\
$F$: $B$ has more heads in the first $n$ tosses \\
$G$: $A$ and $B$ have equal heads in the first $n$ tosses \\
$H$: $A$ gets a head in $(n+1)^{th}$ toss

By law of total probability, $P(E) + P(F) + P(G) = 1$. By symmetry, $P(E) = P(F)$, so:
\begin{equation}
2P(E) + P(G) = 1
\end{equation}

We require the probability that $A$ has more heads than $B$ after $n+1$ tosses. This occurs if $E$ happens (regardless of $H$) OR if $G$ happens AND $H$ happens:
\[ P(\text{Result}) = P(E) + P(G)P(H) \]

Since $P(H) = \frac{1}{2}$, we have:
\[ P(\text{Result}) = P(E) + \frac{1}{2}P(G) = \frac{2P(E) + P(G)}{2} \]

Substituting from (1), we get $P(\text{Result}) = \frac{1}{2}$
\end{solution}

\begin{question}
A, B and C toss a fair coin and get their first heads in $N_A$, $N_B$ and $N_C$ tosses
respectively.
Find $P(N_A < N_B < N_C)$.
\end{question}
\begin{solution}
Let D be the event that $N_A \neq N_B \neq N_C$. By symmetry, each of the 6 permutations under the
event D are equally likely:
\[P(N_A < N_B < N_C) = \frac{1}{6} \times P(D)\]
\begin{equation}
  P(D) = 1 - 3 \times P(two\:are\:same) - P(three\:are\:same)
\end{equation}
$N_A$, $N_B$ and $N_C$ follow the geometric distribution, $(1/2)^n$ where n=1,2... so,
\[P(two\:are\:same) = \sum_{k = 1}^{n}{(\frac{1}{2})^{2n}(1 - (\frac{1}{2})^n)} = 
\sum_{k = 1}^{n}{(\frac{1}{2})^{2n}} - \sum_{k = 1}^{n}{(\frac{1}{2})^{3n}}
= \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{7} = \frac{4}{21}\]\[
P(three\:are\:same) = \sum_{k = 1}^{n}{(\frac{1}{2})^{3n}} = \frac{1}{7}\]
Substituting the above values in (2),
\[
P(D) = 1 - 3 \times \frac{4}{21} - \frac{1}{7} = \frac{2}{7} \text{ hence, }
P(N_A < N_B < N_C) = \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{2}{7} = \frac{1}{21}
\]
\end{solution}

\begin{question}
There are $2^n$ players in a knockount style chess tournament, numbered 1,2,3 . . . $2^n$. 
Player numbered $i$ always wins against player number $j\:\forall j > i$.
What is the probability that the $k^{th}$ best player comes second?
\end{question}
\begin{solution}
W.L.O.G, consider this tournament as a pool style tournament. Let $N = 2^n$ and consider two
pools of $N/2$ players each. The tournament progresses separately in both pools and the final is 
played between the champions from each. Let $P_k$ denote the required probability.

It is evident that $P_k = 0 \: \forall k > N/2$ as this $k^{th}$ best player can never emerge as the
champion from a pool which is the requisite condition for being second.

Also, $P_1$ = 0 as he would always come first.

When $1 < k \leq N/2$, the experiment can be thought of as follows: consider any permutation of $N$ 
players in which the first $N/2$ belong to pool 1 and the rest to pool 2. $P_k$ is the probability 
of the event where players 1,2,..$k-1$ remain in one pool and the $k^{th}$ player remains is the other:
\[
P_k = 1 \times \frac{(N/2) - 1}{N - 1} \times \frac{(N/2) - 2}{N - 2}\:.\:.\:.\:\times
\frac{(N/2) - (k - 2)}{N - (k - 2)} \times \frac{(N/2)}{N - (k-1)}
\]

e.g. when $N = 16$ and $k = 2$, the answer is $\frac{8}{15}$ and when $k=3$, the answer
is $\frac{7}{15} \times \frac{8}{14} = \frac{4}{15}$
\end{solution}

\section*{Counting By Reflection}
\begin{question}
38 people waiting to see a tennis match. The entrance fee is \$5 to enter.
19 of the people only have \$10 bills, 
while the other 19 only have \$5 bills. The cashier currently has no change.
If the 38 people arrange themselves in line completely at random, 
what is the probability that all of the people can successfully purchase a ticket without 
reordering in line?
\end{question}
\begin{solution}
Consider the $P \times Q$ grid in which the x-axis denotes a \$5 bill and the y-axis denotes
a \$10 bill.

We want to count all paths from $(0, 0)$ to $(P, Q)$ where the y co-ordinate never exceeds the
x co-ordinate i.e. 
\begin{equation}
  \text{\#(total paths) $-$ \#(bad paths : paths that touch or cross the line $y = x + 1$)}
\end{equation}

Let Q' be a bad path. The portion of Q' between $(0, 0)$ and the first point of contact
with the line $y = x + 1$ can be reflected about the same line
to give a path from $(-1, 1)$ to $(P, Q)$.

% diagram
\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.6]
    % 1. Setup Grid and Axes
    \draw[step=1, gray!20, thin] (-2,-1) grid (7,7);
    \draw[->, thick] (-2,0) -- (8,0) node[right] {+5s ($x$)};
    \draw[->, thick] (0,-1) -- (0,8) node[above] {+10s ($y$)};

    % 2. Draw Boundaries
    % Blue = Allowed line y=x. Red = Forbidden boundary y=x+1.
    \draw[blue, ultra thin] (0,0) -- (7,7) node[right] {$y = x$};
    \draw[red, ultra thick, dashed] (-2,-1) -- (6,7) node[pos=0.9, sloped, above] {Forbidden: $y = x + 1$};

    % 3. The "Bad" Path (from (0,0) to (6,6))
    % This path touches y=x+1 at (0,1).
    % Path breakdown: (0,0) -> (0,1) -> (1,1) -> (1,4) -> (3,4) -> (3,5) -> (5,5) -> (5,6) -> (6,6)
    \draw[orange, ultra thick] (0,0) -- (0,1) -- (1,1) -- (1,4) -- (3,4) -- (3,5) -- (5,5) -- (5,6) -- (6,6);
    
    % 4. First Contact Point (where bad path hits y=x+1)
    \filldraw[red] (0,1) circle (5pt) node[above left] {Contact};

    % 5. The "Reflected" Segment (from (-1,1) to (0,1))
    % Reflection maps (0,0) to (-1,1).
    % Segment from (-1,1) to (0,1) replaces the (0,0) to (0,1) segment.
    \draw[purple, ultra thick, dotted] (-1,1) -- (0,1);

    % 6. Label Critical Points
    \filldraw[black] (0,0) circle (4pt) node[below right] {$A(0,0)$};
    \filldraw[purple] (-1,1) circle (4pt) node[left] {$A'(-1,1)$};
    \filldraw[black] (6,6) circle (4pt) node[right] {$B(6,6)$};

\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Similarly, any path from $(-1, 1)$ to $(P, Q)$ can be converted to a bad path.

There is a one-one correspondence between the set of bad paths and the set of paths from
$(-1, 1)$ to $(P, Q)$.

Number of paths from $(-1, 1)$ to $(P, Q)$ is equal to ${P + Q \choose Q - 1} = \text{\#(bad paths)}$

Substituting in (3),
\[
\text{\#(good paths)} = {P+Q \choose Q} - {P+Q \choose Q-1} = \frac{1}{Q+1} {P+Q \choose Q}
\]

Dividing by the total number of paths, we get the required probability = $\frac{1}{Q+1}$.

Since here $P=Q=19$, the answer is $\frac{1}{20}$.
\end{solution}

\section*{Optimal Stopping Problems}
\begin{question}
Consider a dice game where upto three rolls of a fair 6 sided die are allowed. The first roll
is free. A player can choose to accept the reward as the face value or forfeit it, pay \$1 and roll
again. The same rule applies for the second roll. In the third roll, there is no choice but to
accept the reward. What is the optimal strategy? What is the expected reward under this optimal
strategy?
\end{question}
\begin{solution}
\textbf{This is an optimal stopping problem where we need to decide the stopping rule for the 
first two rolls.}


The stopping decision for any roll should depend on the future potential, as there is no reward
from the past rolls. Due to this observation, we need to approach this problem via
\textbf{backward induction}.

Let $E_1 \: E_2 \: and\: E_3$ denote the potential reward from starting the game at rolls 1, 2 and
3 respectively.

$E_3 = 3.5 - 1$ = 2.5 where 3.5 is the expected reward and 1 for the fee of rolling the die a 
third time.

This means, at roll 2, we must not accept anything less than 2.5. Stopping decision for roll 2 is
to stop if we roll 3, 4, 5 or 6, or, roll again otherwise. This gives:

\[
E_2 = (\frac{2}{6} \times 2.5) + (\frac{4}{6} \times \frac{3 + 4 + 5 + 6}{4}) - 1 = 2.833 
\]

This means, at roll 1, me accept  3, 4, 5 or 6 and roll again otherwise, which gives:

\[
E_1 = (\frac{4}{6} \times \frac{3 + 4 + 5 + 6}{4}) + (\frac{2}{6} \times 2.833) = 3.944
\]

$\therefore$ the expected reward under optimal strategy is 3.944.
\end{solution}

\section*{Linearity Of Expectation}
\begin{question}
A shuffled deck of 50 black and 50 red cards is laid out linearly. What is the expected number of
runs?
\end{question}
\begin{solution}
Define the indicator variables $I_j$ for $j \in \{1, 2, \dots, 100\}$ such that $I_j = 1$ if a new 
run begins at position $j$, and $I_j = 0$ otherwise.

The total number of runs $R$ is given by:
\[ R = I_1 + \sum_{j=2}^{100} I_j \]

By linearity of expectation:
\[ E[R] = E[I_1] + \sum_{j=2}^{100} E[I_j] \]

$E[I_1] = 1$ and by symmetry, $E[I_i] = 2 \times \frac{50}{100} \times \frac{50}{99}$. Hence,

\[
E[R] = 1 + 99 \times 2 \times \frac{50}{100} \times \frac{50}{99} = 1 + 50 = 51
\]
\end{solution}
